# Critical Appraisal

## Purpose

Critical appraisal is the systematic assessment of research evidence to judge its trustworthiness, results, and relevance to clinical practice. This skill provides structured frameworks for appraising the most common study designs using the Critical Appraisal Skills Programme (CASP) methodology and related tools.

## The Three Fundamental Questions

Every critical appraisal addresses:

1. **Is the study valid?** (Internal validity — is the methodology sound?)
2. **What are the results?** (Magnitude and precision of the effect)
3. **Are the results applicable to my patient?** (External validity — can I generalize?)

## Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT) Appraisal

### Validity Assessment

**1. Was the assignment to treatment groups truly random?**
- Computer-generated random sequence (adequate)
- Alternation, date of birth, hospital number (inadequate — quasi-randomization)
- Allocation concealment (sealed opaque envelopes, central randomization) is distinct from randomization itself and equally important

**2. Were all participants accounted for at the conclusion?**
- Intention-to-treat (ITT) analysis preserves the benefits of randomization
- Per-protocol analysis introduces selection bias
- Loss to follow-up >20% threatens validity
- Were losses balanced between groups?

**3. Were participants, clinicians, and assessors blinded?**
- Double-blind (participant + clinician) — ideal for subjective outcomes
- Assessor blinding is critical for outcomes requiring judgment
- Open-label designs are acceptable when blinding is impractical (e.g., surgical vs medical) but require blinded outcome assessment

**4. Were groups similar at baseline?**
- Table 1 comparison of key prognostic factors
- Statistically significant imbalances in important variables suggest randomization failure or small sample size
- Large trials are more likely to achieve balanced groups

**5. Were groups treated equally apart from the intervention?**
- Co-interventions balanced between groups
- Follow-up intensity and measurement equal
- Contamination (control group receiving intervention) assessed

### Results Interpretation

- **Effect size**: Relative risk, odds ratio, hazard ratio, mean difference
- **Precision**: 95% confidence interval width. Does it cross the null (1.0 for ratios, 0 for differences)?
- **Statistical significance**: p-value context (not a measure of clinical importance)
- **Clinical significance**: Is the effect size large enough to matter clinically? Calculate NNT.

## Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis Appraisal

### Validity Assessment

**1. Was there a clearly focused question?** (PICO defined)

**2. Was the search comprehensive?**
- Multiple databases (PubMed, Embase, CENTRAL at minimum)
- Grey literature searched (conference abstracts, trial registries, theses)
- No language restrictions
- Reference list screening
- Search strategy reproducible and documented

**3. Were inclusion/exclusion criteria appropriate and applied consistently?**
- Dual independent screening
- Agreement statistics reported (kappa)

**4. Was study quality assessed?**
- Risk of bias tool appropriate to study design (RoB 2 for RCTs, ROBINS-I for non-randomized)
- Quality assessment used in synthesis (sensitivity analysis excluding high-risk studies)

**5. Was data synthesis appropriate?**
- Heterogeneity assessed (I², Cochran's Q, prediction interval)
- If I² >50%, investigate sources of heterogeneity (subgroup analysis, meta-regression)
- Random-effects model when heterogeneity is present
- Fixed-effects model when studies are homogeneous
- Publication bias assessed (funnel plot, Egger's test)

### Results Interpretation

- **Pooled effect estimate** with 95% CI
- **Forest plot** reading: individual study weights, point estimates, confidence intervals, diamond (pooled estimate)
- **Heterogeneity**: I² (0-40% low, 30-60% moderate, 50-90% substantial, 75-100% considerable — note overlapping ranges reflect uncertainty)
- **GRADE certainty** for each outcome

## Cohort Study Appraisal

### Key Validity Questions

1. Was the cohort recruited in an acceptable way? (Representative, defined at a common point in disease course)
2. Was exposure accurately measured to minimize bias? (Prospective measurement superior to retrospective)
3. Was the outcome accurately measured? (Blinded assessment, objective outcomes)
4. Were important confounders identified and accounted for? (Multivariable adjustment, propensity scores)
5. Was follow-up long enough and complete enough? (>80% retention, equal follow-up between groups)

### Specific Concerns

- **Healthy worker effect** in occupational cohorts
- **Immortal time bias** in pharmacoepidemiologic studies
- **Residual confounding** — adjustment can reduce but never eliminate confounding in observational studies

## Case-Control Study Appraisal

### Key Validity Questions

1. Were cases and controls clearly defined? (Validated case definition, incident vs prevalent cases)
2. Were controls representative of the source population? (Same catchment, same time period)
3. Was exposure measured in the same way for cases and controls? (Recall bias is the cardinal threat)
4. Were confounders identified and controlled? (Matching, multivariable analysis)

### Specific Concerns

- **Recall bias** — Cases may remember exposures differently than controls
- **Selection bias** — Hospital-based controls may not represent the source population
- **Temporality** — Difficult to establish that exposure preceded outcome in retrospective designs

## Diagnostic Study Appraisal

### Key Validity Questions (STARD Criteria)

1. Was there a valid reference standard? (Gold standard applied to all participants)
2. Was the reference standard applied independently of the index test? (No incorporation bias)
3. Was there a representative spectrum of patients? (Not extreme cases vs healthy controls)
4. Was the index test interpreted without knowledge of the reference standard result? (Blinded interpretation)
5. Were uninterpretable or indeterminate results reported?
6. Was there verification bias? (Partial verification — only positive index tests get the reference standard)

### Results Interpretation

- Sensitivity, specificity, positive and negative likelihood ratios
- Predictive values (depend on prevalence — report with caution)
- ROC curve and AUC for continuous tests
- Spectrum effect — sensitivity and specificity vary with disease severity in the study population

## Qualitative Study Appraisal

### Key Validity Questions

1. Was there a clear statement of aims and research question?
2. Was a qualitative methodology appropriate? (Exploring experiences, beliefs, processes)
3. Was the research design appropriate? (Phenomenology, grounded theory, ethnography, thematic analysis)
4. Was the recruitment strategy appropriate? (Purposive sampling, theoretical sampling, data saturation)
5. Was data collection adequate? (Method, setting, saturation)
6. Was the researcher-participant relationship considered? (Reflexivity)
7. Were ethical issues addressed? (Consent, confidentiality, IRB)
8. Was data analysis rigorous? (Systematic, transparent, multiple coders, respondent validation)

### Qualitative-Specific Quality Markers

- **Thick description** — Rich, contextualized findings
- **Transferability** — Can findings apply to other settings?
- **Dependability** — Would another researcher reach similar conclusions?
- **Confirmability** — Are findings grounded in the data rather than researcher assumptions?

## Rapid Appraisal Checklist

For time-pressured settings, assess these minimum critical elements:

1. Is the study design appropriate for the question?
2. Was the sample adequate (size, representativeness)?
3. Are the results clinically significant (not just statistically significant)?
4. Can I apply these results to my patient?

## Applying This Skill

When appraising evidence:

- Identify the study design before selecting the appraisal framework
- Focus on threats to validity that could change the conclusions (not every methodological limitation is equally important)
- Distinguish statistical significance from clinical significance
- Assess applicability to the specific clinical question and patient population
- Present findings with their limitations clearly stated
