# Literature Review

## Overview

Systematically search and synthesize existing literature before designing experiments. This prevents reinventing the wheel, informs experimental design choices, and identifies knowledge gaps your research can fill.

**Core principle:** Know what's already known before you start.

## When to Use

- After `hypothesis-formulation`, before `experimental-design`
- When the hypothesis touches on an area you're not deeply familiar with
- When comparing your results to prior work

## Process

1. **Define search terms** — Key genes/proteins, pathways, organisms, methods
2. **Search databases** — PubMed, bioRxiv/medRxiv, Google Scholar
3. **Screen results** — Title/abstract scan for relevance
4. **Extract key findings** — Methods used, results, conclusions
5. **Identify gaps** — What hasn't been done? What conflicts exist?
6. **Summarize** — Present findings to user in structured format

## Search Strategy

```markdown
## Search: [Topic]

### Databases Searched
- [ ] PubMed: [query]
- [ ] bioRxiv: [query]
- [ ] Google Scholar: [query]

### Key Papers Found
| # | Title | Authors | Year | Key Finding | Relevance |
|---|-------|---------|------|-------------|-----------|
| 1 | ... | ... | ... | ... | High/Med/Low |

### Summary of Known Knowledge
- [What is established]
- [What is debated/conflicting]
- [What is unknown — the gap]

### Implications for Our Hypothesis
- [How prior work supports/contradicts our hypothesis]
- [Methods others have used that we should consider]
- [Pitfalls others have encountered]
```

## Key Principles

- **Systematic, not anecdotal** — Don't just cite the first paper you find
- **Recent + seminal** — Include both landmark papers and recent work
- **Conflicts are important** — Contradictory findings inform experimental design
- **Don't over-review** — This is to inform the experiment, not write a review paper. Scale to the question.
