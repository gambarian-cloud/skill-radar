# Shared Decision-Making

## Purpose

Shared decision-making (SDM) is a collaborative process in which clinicians and patients make healthcare decisions together, integrating the best available evidence with the patient's values, preferences, and circumstances. SDM is the ethical and clinical standard for preference-sensitive decisions — those where more than one reasonable option exists.

## When SDM Is Essential

### Preference-Sensitive Decisions

Decisions where the "right" choice depends on patient values:

- **Screening** — PSA screening, mammography timing, lung cancer screening eligibility
- **Treatment options** — Surgery vs medical management (e.g., stable CAD, early-stage prostate cancer), anticoagulation in AF with borderline risk
- **End-of-life care** — Goals of care, code status, dialysis initiation in elderly
- **Chronic disease management** — Insulin initiation, statin therapy for primary prevention
- **Risk trade-offs** — Anticoagulation benefit vs bleeding risk, immunosuppression efficacy vs infection risk

### When SDM Is Less Applicable

- **Medical emergencies** — STEMI, tension pneumothorax, anaphylaxis (implied consent for life-saving treatment)
- **Single best option** — When evidence clearly favors one approach with minimal trade-offs
- **Public health mandates** — Quarantine, mandatory reporting (though engagement remains important)

## SDM Frameworks

### Three-Talk Model (Elwyn et al., 2017)

The most widely cited SDM framework:

**1. Team Talk** — Establish partnership

- "Let's work together on this decision"
- Signal that a decision needs to be made
- Offer to collaborate and support deliberation
- Normalize that there is more than one reasonable option

**2. Option Talk** — Compare alternatives

- Describe all reasonable options including "doing nothing" or watchful waiting
- Present benefits and harms of each using absolute numbers
- Use decision aids when available
- Check understanding

**3. Decision Talk** — Reach a decision

- Explore what matters most to the patient
- Support deliberation (allow time, offer deferral if appropriate)
- Confirm the decision and document it
- Arrange implementation and follow-up

### Ottawa Decision Support Framework (ODSF)

A comprehensive framework emphasizing decisional needs assessment:

1. **Assess decisional needs**: Knowledge, values clarity, support, decisional conflict
2. **Provide decision support**: Facts, values clarification, guidance, coaching
3. **Evaluate outcomes**: Decision quality, decisional conflict, values-choice agreement, implementation

The ODSF underpins the development of Ottawa Patient Decision Aids and the Decisional Conflict Scale (DCS).

### SHARE Approach (AHRQ)

Five-step process for clinicians:

1. **S**eek your patient's participation
2. **H**elp your patient explore and compare options
3. **A**ssess your patient's values and preferences
4. **R**each a decision with your patient
5. **E**valuate your patient's decision

## Communicating Risks and Benefits

### Absolute vs Relative Risk

Always use absolute risk. Relative risk exaggerates perceived benefit:

- **Misleading**: "This drug reduces your risk by 50%!" (RRR)
- **Informative**: "Without treatment, 4 in 100 people will have a stroke in 5 years. With treatment, 2 in 100 will. So treatment prevents about 2 strokes per 100 people treated." (ARR)

### Number Needed to Treat (NNT) / Number Needed to Harm (NNH)

NNT makes treatment effects tangible:

- "We need to treat 50 people with this statin for 5 years to prevent one heart attack"
- "For every 200 people treated, one will develop a significant muscle problem"

### Visual Aids for Risk Communication

- **Icon arrays** (100 or 1000 person diagrams) — Most effective for communicating risk
- **Bar charts** comparing treatment vs no treatment
- **Survival curves** for prognostic discussions
- **Frequency framing** — "3 out of 100" rather than "3%"

Avoid:

- Relative risk alone
- Odds ratios (clinically unintuitive)
- Single-event probabilities ("there's a 3% chance") without comparison to alternatives

### Framing Effects

How information is presented influences decisions:

- **Positive frame**: "95% survival rate" — patients more likely to choose treatment
- **Negative frame**: "5% mortality rate" — patients more likely to decline
- **Best practice**: Present both frames and let the patient integrate them

## Decision Aids

### Definition

Structured tools designed to help patients participate in decisions by providing evidence-based information about options, outcomes, and probabilities, and by clarifying personal values.

### Quality Criteria (IPDAS)

The International Patient Decision Aid Standards include:

- Systematic development process
- Up-to-date evidence base
- Balanced presentation of options
- Outcome probabilities using absolute numbers
- Values clarification exercise
- Guidance in deliberation (not persuasion)
- Disclosure of funding and conflicts of interest

### Where to Find Decision Aids

- Ottawa Hospital Research Institute Decision Aid Library
- NICE patient decision aids
- Mayo Clinic Shared Decision Making National Resource Center
- Cochrane Review of decision aids (Stacey et al.) — demonstrates consistent reduction in decisional conflict, improved knowledge, more realistic expectations

## Values Clarification

### Methods

- **Explicit values clarification exercises**: Rating or ranking what matters most (e.g., "How important is avoiding surgery vs having the quickest recovery?")
- **Trade-off questions**: "Would you prefer a treatment with a small risk of a serious side effect, or a less effective treatment with no serious risks?"
- **Deliberation support**: Allow time, offer to involve family, provide written summaries to review at home

### Common Value Domains in Healthcare

- Longevity vs quality of life
- Symptom control vs avoiding side effects
- Independence vs safety
- Certainty vs avoiding procedures
- Short-term inconvenience vs long-term benefit

## Measuring SDM Quality

### OPTION Scale (Observing Patient Involvement)

A validated 12-item instrument for objectively measuring the extent of SDM in clinical consultations:

- Scored by trained observers reviewing recorded consultations
- Items include: identifying the decision, explaining options, exploring patient expectations, checking understanding
- OPTION-5 is a shortened 5-item version with improved reliability

### Decisional Conflict Scale (DCS)

Patient-reported measure of decision quality:

- 16 items across 5 subscales: uncertainty, informed, values clarity, support, effective decision
- Scores >25/100 associated with decision delay or regret
- Scores <25/100 associated with implementing decisions

### CollaboRATE

Three-item patient-reported SDM measure suitable for routine clinical use:

1. How much effort was made to help you understand your health issues?
2. How much effort was made to listen to the things that matter most to you?
3. How much effort was made to include what matters most to you in choosing what to do next?

## Barriers to SDM

- **Time pressure** — SDM can be done efficiently with practice and decision aids
- **Clinician paternalism** — "I know what's best" assumption
- **Patient deference** — "Whatever you think, doctor" — explore further, this may mask unvoiced preferences
- **Health literacy** — Use plain language, visual aids, teach-back
- **Cultural factors** — Some patients prefer family or community-based decision-making
- **Cognitive impairment** — Involve surrogates while maximizing patient involvement

## Applying This Skill

When assisting with preference-sensitive decisions:

- Present all reasonable options including non-intervention
- Use absolute numbers for risks and benefits
- Identify the key value trade-offs
- Suggest relevant decision aids when available
- Frame information in balanced terms (both positive and negative frames)
- Acknowledge uncertainty in the evidence and flag where patient values should guide the decision
