## How to use this appraisal tool

Three broad issues need to be considered when appraising a systematic review study:

1.  **Are the results of the study valid?** (Section A)
2.  **What are the results?** (Section B)
3.  **Will the results help locally?** (Section C)

The 10 questions on the following pages are designed to help you think about these issues systematically. The first two questions are screening questions and can be answered quickly. If the answer to both is "yes", it is worth proceeding with the remaining questions. There is some degree of overlap between the questions, you are asked to record a "yes", "no" or "can't tell" to most of the questions. A number of italicised prompts are given after each question. These are designed to remind you why the question is important. Record your reasons for your answers in the spaces provided.

### About

These checklists were designed to be used as educational pedagogic tools, as part of a workshop setting, therefore we do not suggest a scoring system. The core CASP checklists (randomised controlled trial & systematic review) were based on JAMA 'Users' guides to the medical literature 1994 (adapted from Guyatt GH, Sackett DL, and Cook DJ), and piloted with health care practitioners.

For each new checklist, a group of experts were assembled to develop and pilot the checklist and the workshop format with which it would be used. Over the years overall adjustments have been made to the format, but a recent survey of checklist users reiterated that the basic format continues to be useful and appropriate.

## Paper for appraisal and reference

**Title:** Type and timing of menopausal hormone therapy and breast cancer risk: individual participant meta-analysis of the worldwide epidemiological evidence

**Summary:**
This is a large-scale study exploring the association between breast cancer and different types of menopausal hormone therapy (MHT). In a global collaboration spanning over 25 years, the authors re-analyzed data collected from 58 large-scale prospective and retrospective epidemiological studies, both published and unpublished. Consistently, they observed elevated breast cancer risks associated with oestrogen-progestagen therapy, compared to oestrogen-only therapy.

## Section A: Are the results of the review valid?

### 1. Did the review address a clearly focused question?

**HINT:** An issue can be 'focused' In terms of:
*   the population studied
*   the intervention given
*   the outcome considered

**Answer:** **[Yes]** / Can't Tell / No

**Comments:**
The meta-analysis addressed the issue of breast cancer risk associated with different types and duration of menopausal hormone therapy (MHT) among women in western countries. It only considers invasive breast cancer incidence.

### 2. Did the authors look for the right type of papers?

**HINT:** 'The best sort of studies' would:
*   address the review's question
*   have an appropriate study design (usually RCTs for papers evaluating interventions)

**Answer:** **[Yes]** / Can't Tell / No

**Comments:**
The authors looked for epidemiological studies, both prospective and retrospective, of MHT. The authors also sought MHT randomized trials.

### 3. Do you think all the important, relevant studies were included?

**HINT:** Look for:
*   which bibliographic databases were used
*   follow up from reference lists
*   personal contact with experts
*   unpublished as well as published studies
*   non-English language studies

**Answer:** Yes / **[Can't Tell]** / No

**Comments:**
The authors claimed that they have searched epidemiological studies of MHT through personal contacts, correspondence with collaborators, searches of review articles, searches of references in journal articles, and in MEDLINE and PubMed. The search terms they used is comprehensive. They also claim to have sought unpublished epidemiological studies on MHT through correspondence with colleagues and discussions at meetings of collaborators. However, a number of large studies were excluded. A large study in France, E3N, is not included (though part of the study is included through EPIC). The E3N study has contrasting conclusions regarding the RR of different combinations of estrogen+prostagen. The authors also sought MHT randomized trials. However, data from these trials are not included in their main analyses due to sample size not meeting criteria. It is unclear why large trials such as WHI trial did not meet criteria. Though, the author displayed results using data from the trials separately.
Studies included in this meta-analysis were from Europe and North America (and one from Australia), which limits the generalizability of the results to non-Western populations. It is unclear whether studies in non-English have been sought out.
A flow chart showing the process of inclusion/exclusion of studies will be useful.

### 4. Did the review's authors do enough to assess quality of the included studies?

**HINT:** The authors need to consider the rigour of the studies they have identified. Lack of rigour may affect the studies' results ("All that glisters is not gold" Merchant of Venice – Act II Scene 7)

**Answer:** **[Yes]** / Can't Tell / No

**Comments:**
The authors defined inclusion criteria of studies, including sample size requirement and the availability of individual information of subjects. Both prospective and retrospective studies were included which potentially introduce heterogeneity. However, this was resolved by analysing prospective and retrospective studies separately. Unpublished studies have not been peer-reviewed, and the rigor of the methodology and data collection could not be evaluated.
The authors have acquired individual data from principal investigators of the studies included. They have assessed the completeness of the data and excluded studies that lack relevant information they sought.

### 5. If the results of the review have been combined, was it reasonable to do so?

**HINT:** Consider whether:
*   results were similar from study to study
*   results of all the included studies are clearly displayed
*   results of different studies are similar
*   reasons for any variations in results are discussed

**Answer:** **[Yes]** / Can't Tell / No

**Comments:**
The authors performed independent analyses on the included studies. The study-specific results showed that retrospective studies generally have a lower RR than prospective studies, especially in studies from the US. They have separated the prospective studies and retrospective studies and analyzed them separately. The authors have collected individual data from each study included and performed their own analysis on the combined dataset. The results from both prospective and retrospective studies are shown. The authors have tried to explain the differences observed between the two types of studies.
The authors did not take the sample size into consideration when performing combined analysis on the prospective studies. Therefore, the results from the analysis may be a reflection of larger studies.

## Section B: What are the results?

### 6. What are the overall results of the review?

**HINT:** Consider:
*   If you are clear about the review's 'bottom line' results
*   what these are (numerically if appropriate)
*   how were the results expressed (NNT, odds ratio etc.)

**Comments:**
The main result is that MHT in postmenopausal women are associated with increased risk of invasive breast cancer incident. The risk increases with the duration of usage and is further increased with estrogen-progestagen combination. For current users, estrogen-only MHT has a RR 1.17 in year 1-4 and RR 1.33 in year 5-14; while estrogen-progestagen combination has a RR 1.6 in year 1-4 and RR 2.08 in year 5-14, of breast cancer compared to MHT never users. Notably, even after MHT was stopped, this duration-dependent risk persisted for more than a decade.

### 7. How precise are the results?

**HINT:** Look at the confidence intervals, if given

**Comments:**
The estimates were mostly precise due to the sheer size of the study. The confidence intervals of estimates were very narrow except in extreme ends of groups (duration <1year, age 44-44, age 60-69).

## Section C: Will the results help locally?

### 8. Can the results be applied to the local population?

**HINT:** Consider whether:
*   the patients covered by the review could be sufficiently different to your population to cause concern
*   your local setting is likely to differ much from that of the review

**Answer:** Yes / Can't Tell / **[No]**

**Comments:**
The results cover mostly women in western countries. Most subjects from the prospective studies in Europe are from UK, which is an overrepresentation of the UK population among Europe countries. The results where estrogen-only MHT is associated with higher breast cancer risk contradicts with results reported in the randomized trials of MHT vs placebo, where estrogen-only MHT reduced the risk of breast cancers. Prospective studies from North America appears to have lower RRs compared to prospective studies from Europe. In addition, no study from non-western countries were included. Therefore, the results cannot be generalized to non-western countries.

### 9. Were all important outcomes considered?

**HINT:** Consider whether:
*   there is other information you would like to have seen

**Answer:** Yes / Can't Tell / **[No]**

**Comments:**
This paper only assessed the risks associated with use of MHT between women of similar age of at menopause. However, it would be meaningful and clinically relevant to compare the breast cancer risks of postmenopausal women with MHT to premenopausal women of similar ages, especially for women in the 40s.
Compared to MHT never users, women taking MHT at/after menopause could be the result of them experiencing health issues. The differences in health conditions between MHT ever users and MHT never users could be confounding factors that contribute to the increased breast cancer risks. Similarly, the attenuated effect for breast cancer in the 60-69 age group may be due to the presence of competing risk apart from HRT. (eg: current chemotherapy or any other drugs from other conditions that could have prevented breast cancer). More treatment, comorbidity, and death information should be provided.

### 10. Are the benefits worth the harms and costs?

**HINT:** Consider:
*   even if this is not addressed by the review, what do you think?

**Answer:** Yes / **[Can't Tell]** / No

**Comments:**
This study provides the epidemiological evidence that MHT is associated with increased risk of invasive breast cancer. Whether to use MHT should be determined in a case-by-case fashion. There is existing evidence that HRT lowers the incidence of endometrial cancer. The results in this analysis for estrogen-only MHT contradicts with those from randomized MHT trials. Careful evaluation of benefit and harm for the specific patient should be carried out and the patients should be fully informed of the risks.
