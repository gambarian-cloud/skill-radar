# CASP Checklist: 12 questions to help you make sense of a Cohort Study

**Paper for appraisal:** The Epidemiological Characteristics of an Outbreak of 2019 Novel Coronavirus Diseases (COVID-19) — China, 2020

**Summary:**
This epidemiological study presents the characteristics of COVID-19 patients in China up to February 2020. It is an important contribution at a time where the world has been preparing for COVID and China had most of the information. This population study provided important knowledge on the demographic differences in COVID positivity and case fatality ratio, and demonstrates the geo-temporal spread of the virus.
However, one major flaw was that a large proportion of cases (37%) were not tested with viral nucleic acid, but were clinically diagnosed. Although most of the analyses focused on the remaining cases that were tested with viral nucleic acid, clinically diagnosed cases form a large subpopulation that could potentially affect the interpretation of the findings, should viral nucleic acid tests on the clinically diagnosed cases return positive results. Further, this is a huge population and biases due to inconsistent definition of cases and potential misclassification subject to individual clinician interpretation may arise. Yet, little information is provided about how data is entered into the database and later on extracted by the study authors.

---

## How to use this appraisal tool

Three broad issues need to be considered when appraising a cohort study:
1. Are the results of the study valid? (Section A)
2. What are the results? (Section B)
3. Will the results help locally? (Section C)

The 12 questions on the following pages are designed to help you think about these issues systematically. The first two questions are screening questions and can be answered quickly. If the answer to both is "yes", it is worth proceeding with the remaining questions.

## Section A: Are the results of the study valid?

### 1. Did the study address a clearly focused issue?

**Answer:** Yes ☑ | Can't Tell ☐ | No ☐

**HINT:** A question can be 'focused' in terms of:
*   the population studied
*   the risk factors studied
*   is it clear whether the study tried to detect a beneficial or harmful effect
*   the outcomes considered

**Comments:**
This was a descriptive, exploratory analysis of COVID-19 cases in China, diagnosed as of 11 Feb 2020. However, it was not clear what the scope of the study was in the abstract or introduction.

### 2. Was the cohort recruited in an acceptable way?

**Answer:** Yes ☑ | Can't Tell ☐ | No ☐

**HINT:** Look for selection bias which might compromise the generalisability of the findings:
*   was the cohort representative of a defined population
*   was there something special about the cohort
*   was everybody included who should have been

**Comments:**
By Chinese law, all COVID-19 related cases are required to be reported to China's Infectious Disease Information System. The current study included all cases reported to this database without sampling and without filtering for eligibility criteria. All case records contain national identification numbers and are not duplicated.

### 3. Was the exposure accurately measured to minimise bias?

**Answer:** Yes ☐ | Can't Tell ☐ | No ☑

**HINT:** Look for measurement or classification bias:
*   did they use subjective or objective measurements
*   do the measurements truly reflect what you want them to (have they been validated)
*   were all the subjects classified into exposure groups using the same procedure

**Comments:**
This study was not meant for research. Several self-reported variables could be susceptible to recall bias, such as date of symptom onset and Wuhan-related exposure.
Further, comorbidities were reported without verification. Given the large proportion of missing comorbidity data (~50% of confirmed cases), there is a high risk of inaccuracy in the comorbidity distribution data. This might affect the CFR for all comorbidity groups. Perhaps a random sample of information on comorbidities could be extracted from electronic medical records, if available, and could be used to verify how accurate the self-reports were.

### 4. Was the outcome accurately measured to minimise bias?

**Answer:** Yes ☑ | Can't Tell ☐ | No ☐

**HINT:** Look for measurement or classification bias:
*   did they use subjective or objective measurements
*   do the measurements truly reflect what you want them to (have they been validated)
*   has a reliable system been established for detecting all the cases (for measuring disease occurrence)
*   were the measurement methods similar in the different groups
*   were the subjects and/or the outcome assessor blinded to exposure (does this matter)

**Comments:**
**COVID-19 positivity:**
Patients were diagnosed with COVID-19 using objective measurements such as the viral nucleic acid test ('confirmed' group), thus reducing the risk of detection bias. Additionally, where the viral nucleic acid test was not available, patients were diagnosed based on symptoms/exposures ('suspected' group), and lung imaging ('clinically diagnosed' group). A group of patients who tested positive on the viral nucleic acid test, but who did not display any COVID-19 symptoms were also classified separately ('asymptomatic' group). Patients in the different groups were properly identified, and most of the data analyses were performed on patients in the confirmed group.
Records of COVID-19 patients were entered into the Infectious Disease Information System by epidemiologists and public health workers who investigated and collected information on possible exposures. These records were then separately extracted by the study authors for analysis. The study included all COVID-19 related case records reported to China's Infectious Disease Information System, thus reducing the risk of sampling and selection bias.

However, the study would benefit from providing more information about the context of reporting. It was not clear under what circumstances would patients be referred for viral nucleic testing. There was no information on whether an official criterion for viral nucleic testing released from the Wuhan Municipal Health Commission or China CDC.

**Deaths:**
Death data was extracted from the Infectious Disease Information System. It was not clear whether the deaths were attributable to COVID-19. Given that 80% of death occurred in cases above 60, an age group with higher rates of comorbidity, it is plausible that these patients died from complications unrelated to COVID-19. Also, the cross-sectional nature of the study subjects mortality to length bias. The authors should extract deaths up to 25 February 2020 to ensure that deaths in later patients are accounted for.

### 5. (a) Have the authors identified all important confounding factors?

**Answer:** Yes ☑ | Can't Tell ☐ | No ☐

**HINT:**
*   list the ones you think might be important, and ones the author missed

**Comments:**
At the time of writing in Feb 2020, not much is known about the novel coronavirus. The authors have collected essential information on time, place and person. Time being the date on onset of symptoms, place being the Wuhan-related exposure and geospatial analysis, person being demographic data and pre-existing illnesses.

### 5. (b) Have they taken account of the confounding factors in the design and/or analysis?

**Answer:** Yes ☐ | Can't Tell ☐ | No ☑

**HINT:**
*   look for restriction in design, and techniques e.g. modelling, stratified-, regression-, or sensitivity analysis to correct, control or adjust for confounding factors

**Comments:**
The authors have provided a descriptive analysis on important epidemiological important variables, including age, sex, comorbidities and its geo-temporal progression. However, there could be interaction effects between variables. For example, one of the main findings was a higher CFR on retirees and those with comorbidities. CFR rate has also been shown to spike from 60 years. The findings from occupation and comorbidities could be an effect simply due to old age.

Further analyses on interaction terms such as age x occupation, case severity x comorbidity, case severity x age, would be useful and possible with the available data. More subgroups should also be considered, such as those divided by severity and the number of comorbidities.
The authors should replicate Table 1 and include columns of suspected, clinically diagnosed, and asymptomatic to compare with confirmed cases against all characteristics as Appendix.

### 6. (a) Was the follow up of subjects complete enough?

**Answer:** Yes ☐ | Can't Tell ☐ | No ☑

**HINT:** Consider
*   the good or bad effects should have had long enough to reveal themselves
*   the persons that are lost to follow-up may have different outcomes than those available for assessment
*   in an open or dynamic cohort, was there anything special about the outcome of the people leaving, or the exposure of the people entering the cohort

### 6. (b) Was the follow up of subjects long enough?

**Answer:** Yes ☐ | Can't Tell ☑ | No ☐

**Comments:**
There was no follow up as this was a cross-sectional study. However, the authors could have provided more information about patient outcomes and symptoms after their records were entered into the database.

## Section B: What are the results?

### 7. What are the results of this study?

**HINT:** Consider
*   what are the bottom line results
*   have they reported the rate or the proportion between the exposed/unexposed, the ratio/rate difference
*   how strong is the association between exposure and outcome (RR)
*   what is the absolute risk reduction (ARR)

**Comments:**
Overall, the authors found that COVID-19 is highly contagious. Majority of the confirmed cases were farmers or laborers, with Wuhan-related exposure. While most of the cases were mild, it was most fatal among male patients, those aged >80, with Wuhan-related exposure and pre-existing comorbid conditions. Half of the cases were critical, and all deaths came from this group.

### 8. How precise are the results?

**HINT:**
*   look for the range of the confidence intervals, if given

**Comments:**
As this is a population-level descriptive study, all data is reported as absolute numbers and percentages. No statistical analysis is performed.

### 9. Do you believe the results?

**Answer:** Yes ☑ | Can't Tell ☐ | No ☐

**HINT:** Consider
*   big effect is hard to ignore
*   can it be due to bias, chance or confounding
*   are the design and methods of this study sufficiently flawed to make the results unreliable
*   Bradford Hills criteria (e.g. time sequence, dose-response gradient, biological plausibility, consistency)

**Comments:**
This is a descriptive study that included all case records available, and most of the analysis were conducted on patients confirmed by an objective measure (viral nucleic testing).
That said, there are missing data that could likely affect the interpretation of results (see Table 1). For example, for Wuhan-related exposure, a significant proportion of data is missing, with the number of missing data (n=7,403) exceeding the number of 'no' responses (n=5295). Large number of missing data were also observed for the comorbid condition, and, to a lesser extent, case severity.
(continued ...)
Further, there was no clarification on the progress of patients and how would that influence the analysis. For example, if a patient progress from suspected to confirmed, did the total number of suspected cases get subtracted by one? The CFR should also be interpreted with caution since the authors have not been clear on the way deaths have been defined. Also, it was not clear why there was a sudden spike in cases around 1 Feb 2020 in Figure 3, why different values were given for the number of healthcare workers with confirmed COVID-19 (1,716 in Table 1, but 1,688 in Table 2), and what the number of confirmed cases in Wuhan is (this information is provided for Hubei in Table 1, pg 4, but not separately for Wuhan). Survival analysis could also be added to show the likelihood of deaths with time. Lastly, it should be noted that this article was published by the Chinese Center for Disease Control and Prevention and may not have undergone the usual journal peer-review process.

## Section C: Will the results help locally?

### 10. Can the results be applied to the local population?

**Answer:** Yes ☑ | Can't Tell ☐ | No ☐

**HINT:** Consider whether
*   a cohort study was the appropriate method to answer this question
*   the subjects covered in this study could be sufficiently different from your population to cause concern
*   your local setting is likely to differ much from that of the study
*   you can quantify the local benefits and harms

**Comments:**
Although this is a descriptive study whose scope is limited to COVID-19 cases reported in China, and the distribution of variables may differ for other populations the results of the studies are largely in line with studies conducted on different populations (see Question 11).

### 11. Do the results of this study fit with other available evidence?

**Answer:** Yes ☑ | Can't Tell ☐ | No ☐

**Comments:**
Yes. This paper, published in February 2020, is likely one of the earliest epidemiological papers on COVID-19. The main findings, that COVID-19 spreads very quickly through human-to-human transmission and are more fatal for the elderly and people with comorbidities are in line with results of later studies on other populations such as the United States (Richardson et al, 2020), United Kingdom (Docherty et al, 2020), and Spain (Poblador-Plou et al, 2020).

**References:**
1. Richardson S, Hirsch JS, Narasimhan M, et al. Presenting Characteristics, Comorbidities, and Outcomes Among 5700 Patients Hospitalized With COVID-19 in the New York City Area. JAMA. 2020;323(20):2052. doi:10.1001/jama.2020.6775
2. Docherty AB, Harrison EM, Green CA, et al. Features of 20 133 UK patients in hospital with covid-19 using the ISARIC WHO Clinical Characterisation Protocol: prospective observational cohort study. BMJ. Published online May 22, 2020:m1985. doi:10.1136/bmj.m1985
3. Poblador-Plou B, Carmona-Pírez J, Ioakeim-Skoufa I, et al. Baseline Chronic Comorbidity and Mortality in Laboratory-Confirmed COVID-19 Cases: Results from the PRECOVID Study in Spain. IJERPH. 2020;17(14):5171. doi:10.3390/ijerph17145171

### 12. What are the implications of this study for practice?

**Answer:** [No selection made]
*NOTE: It's not proper to say YES/NO for implication here.*

**HINT:** Consider
*   one observational study rarely provides sufficiently robust evidence to recommend changes to clinical practice or within health policy decision making
*   for certain questions, observational studies provide the only evidence
*   recommendations from observational studies are always stronger when supported by other evidence

**Comments:**
This study demonstrates that COVID-19 disproportionately affects the elderly population and patients with certain comorbidities, suggesting that such populations should be provided a higher level of care. Further, the rapid spread of the virus through the population underscores the need for measures limiting human-to-human transmission, such as mask wearing, frequent hand washing, and even the isolation of entire cities.
