# CASP Checklist: Randomised Controlled Trial

**Paper for appraisal and reference:** Polypill for Cardiovascular Disease Prevention in an Underserved Population

---

## General Information

**How to use this appraisal tool:** Three broad issues need to be considered when appraising a trial:
1. Are the results of the study valid? (Section A)
2. What are the results? (Section B)
3. Will the results help locally? (Section C)

The 11 questions on the following pages are designed to help you think about these issues systematically. The first three questions are screening questions and can be answered quickly. If the answer to both is "yes", it is worth proceeding with the remaining questions.

**About:** These checklists were designed to be used as educational pedagogic tools, as part of a workshop setting, therefore we do not suggest a scoring system. The core CASP checklists (randomised controlled trial & systematic review) were based on JAMA 'Users' guides to the medical literature 1994 (adapted from Guyatt GH, Sackett DL, and Cook DJ), and piloted with health care practitioners.

## Section A: Are the results of the trial valid?

### 1. Did the trial address a clearly focused issue?

**HINT:** An issue can be 'focused' In terms of:
*   the population studied
*   the intervention given
*   the comparator given
*   the outcomes considered

**Answer:** ✅ **Yes** | ☐ Can't Tell | ☐ No

**Comments:**
The population is adults without cardiovascular disease in a socioeconomically vulnerable minority population.
The intervention is use of polypill comparing with usual care.
The "primary" outcomes are the changes in systolic blood pressure and LDL cholesterol level from baseline to 12 months.

### 2. Was the assignment of patients to treatments randomised?

**HINT:** Consider:
*   how this was carried out
*   was the allocation sequence concealed from researchers and patients

**Answer:** ☐ Yes | ✅ **Can't Tell** | ☐ No

**Comments:**
Randomization was done by dividing eligible group into two group. Those are polypill treatment and usual case treatment. On other aspect, there is no detail description of how the randomisation was done. The allocation sequence generation and allocation sequence concealment are not described in the paper as the authors just mentioned eligible participants are who provided written informed consent were randomly assigned to receive either the polypill or usual care.

### 3. Were all of the patients who entered the trial properly accounted for at its conclusion?

**HINT:** Consider:
*   was the trial stopped early
*   were patients analysed in the groups to which they were randomised

**Answer:** ✅ **Yes** | ☐ Can't Tell | ☐ No

**Comments:**
Figure 1 shows the progress of patients through the trial. 20 discontinued trial in the polypill group and 8 discontinued trial in the usual-care group. Though the authors didn't mention whether they used intention-to-treat (ITT) analysis or per-protocol analysis in the Methods Section, results in Table 2 represents the per-protocol analysis and ITT analysis are reported using multiple imputation in p.1119. Both analyses yielded similar results.

> **Is it worth continuing?** (Implied Yes based on continuation)

### 4. Were patients, health workers and study personnel 'blind' to treatment?

**Answer:** ☐ Yes | ☐ Can't Tell | ✅ **No**

**Comments:**
No. It is an open-label study. The patients, health workers and study personnel know which treatment is being administered. Further, it's not possible to run this study in a blinded manner

### 5. Were the groups similar at the start of the trial?

**HINT:** Consider:
*   other factors that might affect the outcome, such as; age, sex, social class

**Answer:** ✅ **Yes** | ☐ Can't Tell | ☐ No

**Comments:**
Only small difference on current smokers but was not statistically significant

### 6. Aside from the experimental intervention, were the groups treated equally?

**Answer:** ☐ Yes | ☐ Can't Tell | ✅ **No**

**Comments:**
The outcome assessment was not done in the blinded manner, thus suggesting detection bias. In this case, outcome assessors (study nurses or investigators) who are aware of the actual treatment may unconsciously or intentionally alter their assessment. The authors described the limitations of the open-label design but did not discuss how these biases could be minimised

## Section B: What are the results?

### 7. How large was the treatment effect?

**HINT:** Consider:
*   what outcomes were measured
*   Is the primary outcome clearly specified
*   what results were found for each outcome

**Comments:**
The systolic blood pressure decreased by 9 mm hg in the polypill group
The systolic blood pressure decreased by 2 mm hg in the usual care group
The difference: - 7 mm hg (95% CI –12 to –2 ) p = 0.003
The LDL cholesterol level decreased by 15 mg per deciliter in the polypill group
The LDL cholesterol level decreased by 4 mg per deciliter in the usual care group
The difference: -11 mg (95 % CI –18 to –5) p < 0.001
The p value were calculated by student t-test and the result reach statistically significant.

### 8. How precise was the estimate of the treatment effect?

**HINT:** Consider:
*   what are the confidence limits

**Comments:**
The estimates are precise even at a 1% significance level. However, the range is quite large. It will help if boxplots were shown to understand the distribution of the primary outcome.

## Section C: Will the results help locally?

### 9. Can the results be applied to the local population, or in your context?

**HINT:** Consider whether:
*   the patients covered by the trial are similar enough to the patients to whom you will apply this
*   how they differ

**Answer:** ☐ Yes | ☐ Can't Tell | ✅ **No**

**Comments:**
Because the sample size is limited to socioeconomically vulnerable minority. Lifestyle and genetic factors may also affect LDL and BP and their response to drugs. In this context, the population also happened to be 96% black. These results may not be generalisable to the low-income White/Hispanic populations.

### 10. Were all clinically important outcomes considered?

**HINT:** Consider whether:
*   there is other information you would like to have seen
*   if not, does this affect the decision

**Answer:** ☐ Yes | ☐ Can't Tell | ✅ **No**

**Comments:**
Though the authors had considered lots of secondary outcome including predicted 10-yr risk of CV disease (%), other clinically important outcomes such number of participants experienced the primary CVD outcome (myocardial infarction, heart failure etc) are missing.

### 11. Are the benefits worth the harms and costs?

**HINT:** Consider:
*   even if this is not addressed by the trial, what do you think?

**Answer:** ☐ Yes | ☐ Can't Tell | ✅ **No**

**Comments:**
The demonstrated benefit of the polypill strategy is modest BP and LDL-C reduction. But the costs associated with the polypill intervention is estimated to be 46,176$ (26$ per month per participant, 12 months and 148 participants). On the other hand, the costs associated with usual care is estimated to be 1860$ (3$ for 90-day supply of medication and 155 participants). Thus, benefits demonstrated for polypill strategy is not worth the costs. Only 1% of polypill patients experienced myalgias and hypotension respectively.
