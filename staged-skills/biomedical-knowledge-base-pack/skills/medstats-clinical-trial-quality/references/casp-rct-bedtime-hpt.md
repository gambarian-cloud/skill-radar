# CASP Checklist: Randomised Controlled Trial

**Paper for appraisal and reference:** Bedtime hypertension treatment improves cardiovascular risk reduction: the Hygia Chronotherapy Trial

---

## Introductory Information

**How to use this appraisal tool:** Three broad issues need to be considered when appraising a trial:
*   Are the results of the study valid? (Section A)
*   What are the results? (Section B)
*   Will the results help locally? (Section C)

The 11 questions on the following pages are designed to help you think about these issues systematically. The first three questions are screening questions and can be answered quickly. If the answer to both is "yes", it is worth proceeding with the remaining questions. There is some degree of overlap between the questions, you are asked to record a "yes", "no" or "can't tell" to most of the questions. A number of italicised prompts are given after each question. These are designed to remind you why the question is important. Record your reasons for your answers in the spaces provided.

**About:** These checklists were designed to be used as educational pedagogic tools, as part of a workshop setting, therefore we do not suggest a scoring system. The core CASP checklists (randomised controlled trial & systematic review) were based on JAMA 'Users' guides to the medical literature 1994 (adapted from Guyatt GH, Sackett DL, and Cook DJ), and piloted with health care practitioners.

**Referencing:** we recommend using the Harvard style citation, i.e.: Critical Appraisal Skills Programme (2018). CASP (insert name of checklist i.e. Randomised Controlled Trial) Checklist. [online] Available at: URL. Accessed: Date Accessed.

## Section A: Are the results of the trial valid?

### 1. Did the trial address a clearly focused issue?

**HINT:** An issue can be 'focused' In terms of
*   the population studied
*   the intervention given
*   the comparator given
*   the outcomes considered

**Answer:** ☑ **Yes** | ☐ Can't Tell | ☐ No

**Comments:**
However, according to ClinicalTrials.gov the primary outcome stated is to evaluate the impact of circadian time of treatment in cardiovascular, cerebrovascular, metabolic, and renal risk assessment

### 2. Was the assignment of patients to treatments randomised?

**HINT:** Consider
*   how this was carried out
*   was the allocation sequence concealed from researchers and patients

**Answer:** ☐ Yes | ☑ **Can't Tell** | ☐ No

**Comments:**
The paper did not describe the randomisation procedure in detail. From a separately found publication, we found that the assignment of hypertensive participants to treatment-time regimen is done using an allocation table constructed at the RCC for each participating center using a computerized random-number generator. Randomization of participants to treatment-time regimen is done separately for each hypertensive medication or combination being tested. However, the allocation sequence concealment are not described. These details should have been included in the manuscript.

### 3. Were all of the patients who entered the trial properly accounted for at its conclusion?

**HINT:** Consider
*   was the trial stopped early
*   were patients analysed in the groups to which they were randomised

**Answer:** ☐ Yes | ☐ Can't Tell | ☑ **No**

**Comments:**
Though the authors mentioned demographic and clinical variables were compared on an intention-to-treat basis, 84 patients lost to follow-up are not included in the final analysis and detailed reasons are not documented in Fig 1.

### 4. Were patients, health workers and study personnel 'blind' to treatment?

**Answer:** ☐ Yes | ☐ Can't Tell | ☑ **No**

**Comments:**
This is an open-label prospective study. Both clinician and patients are aware of the regiments and allowed to prescribe drugs freely. However, as its name implied, the study end points are adjudicated by an independent committee that is unaware of the treatment allocation to guarantee the unbiased comparison of therapies and evaluation of study results.

### 5. Were the groups similar at the start of the trial?

**HINT:** Consider
*   other factors that might affect the outcome, such as; age, sex, social class

**Answer:** ☐ Yes | ☑ **Can't Tell** | ☐ No

**Comments:**
The severity of hypertension, drug and dosage information at baseline are missing. These are important information pertaining to the final outcome that should be included. However, there were another a number of variables that were statistically significant (p<0.05). The authors should provide more details of the distribution of the variables (eg: histograms, boxplots).

### 6. Aside from the experimental intervention, were the groups treated equally?

**Answer:** ☑ **Yes** | ☐ Can't Tell | ☐ No

**Comments:**
However, this is dependent on the baseline characteristics such as disease severity, baseline medications and dosage and any changes to it. In terms of outcome assessment, the study was carried out in a blinded manner.

## Section B: What are the results?

### 7. How large was the treatment effect?

**HINT:** Consider
*   what outcomes were measured
*   Is the primary outcome clearly specified
*   what results were found for each outcome

**Comments:**
Treatment results in an adjusted hazard ratio of 0.55 [95% CI: 0.5 - 0.61] for primary CVD outcome.

### 8. How precise was the estimate of the treatment effect?

**HINT:** Consider
*   what are the confidence limits

**Comments:**
The results are precise for the primary CVD outcome.

## Section C: Will the results help locally?

### 9. Can the results be applied to the local population, or in your context?

**HINT:** Consider whether
*   the patients covered by the trial are similar enough to the patients to whom you will apply this
*   how they differ

**Answer:** ☐ Yes | ☐ Can't Tell | ☑ **No**

**Comments:**
It is difficult to tell whether this method will work on another population with different clinical guidelines for hypertension treatment. Also, there is doubt about whether their measure for medication adherence is appropriate and applicable.

### 10. Were all clinically important outcomes considered?

**HINT:** Consider whether
*   there is other information you would like to have seen
*   if not, does this affect the decision

**Answer:** ☑ **Yes** | ☐ Can't Tell | ☐ No

**Comments:**

### 11. Are the benefits worth the harms and costs?

**HINT:** Consider
*   even if this is not addressed by the trial, what do you think?

**Answer:** ☑ **Yes** | ☐ Can't Tell | ☐ No

**Comments:**
In general, yes, as the occurrence of CVD events significantly reduced. However, the harms of conducting bedtime hypertension treatment is unclear and untested, despite the fact that the cost is marginal.
The study shows that by changing only the time of administration of hypertension drugs, the hazard ratio of CVD events is significantly reduced. However, the conclusion of the study is not well corroborated by studies from other groups.
