# Evidence Levels and Hierarchies

## Purpose

Evidence classification systems provide a common language for describing the strength and quality of clinical evidence. This skill covers the major systems in use, how they relate to each other, and their important limitations.

## Traditional Evidence Hierarchy (Pyramid)

The classic hierarchy ranks study designs by their inherent susceptibility to bias:

1. **Systematic reviews and meta-analyses** of RCTs
2. **Randomized controlled trials** (RCTs)
3. **Cohort studies** (prospective > retrospective)
4. **Case-control studies**
5. **Case series and case reports**
6. **Expert opinion / Mechanism-based reasoning**

### Rationale

Each level has progressively less control over confounding and bias. Randomization eliminates selection bias and balances unmeasured confounders. Systematic reviews aggregate evidence and increase precision.

### Important Limitations of the Hierarchy

- A well-conducted cohort study may provide stronger evidence than a poorly conducted RCT
- RCTs are not always feasible or ethical (surgical procedures, rare diseases, long-term harms)
- The hierarchy addresses internal validity but not external validity or applicability
- Some questions are better answered by observational designs (harm, prognosis, diagnosis)
- N-of-1 trials and pragmatic designs do not fit neatly into the pyramid

## Oxford Centre for Evidence-Based Medicine (CEBM) Levels

The Oxford CEBM system (2011 revision) is question-type specific, recognizing that the optimal study design depends on the clinical question.

### Therapy / Prevention / Harm

| Level | Study Type |
|---|---|
| 1a | SR of RCTs (with homogeneity) |
| 1b | Individual RCT (with narrow CI) |
| 1c | All-or-none case series |
| 2a | SR of cohort studies (with homogeneity) |
| 2b | Individual cohort study (or low-quality RCT) |
| 2c | Outcomes research; ecological studies |
| 3a | SR of case-control studies |
| 3b | Individual case-control study |
| 4 | Case series, poor-quality cohort/case-control |
| 5 | Expert opinion without explicit critical appraisal |

### Diagnosis

| Level | Study Type |
|---|---|
| 1a | SR of Level 1 diagnostic studies (with homogeneity) |
| 1b | Validating cohort with good reference standard |
| 1c | Absolute SpPins and SnNouts |
| 2a | SR of Level 2+ diagnostic studies |
| 2b | Exploratory cohort with good reference standard |
| 3b | Non-consecutive study or without consistently applied reference standard |
| 4 | Case-control study or poor/non-independent reference standard |
| 5 | Expert opinion |

### Prognosis

| Level | Study Type |
|---|---|
| 1a | SR of inception cohort studies |
| 1b | Individual inception cohort with >80% follow-up |
| 1c | All-or-none case series |
| 2a | SR of retrospective cohort studies or untreated control groups in RCTs |
| 2b | Retrospective cohort study or follow-up of untreated control group in RCT |
| 2c | Outcomes research |
| 4 | Case series or poor-quality prognostic cohort |
| 5 | Expert opinion |

### Grades of Recommendation (Oxford CEBM)

- **A** — Consistent Level 1 studies
- **B** — Consistent Level 2 or 3 studies, or extrapolation from Level 1
- **C** — Level 4 studies or extrapolation from Level 2 or 3
- **D** — Level 5 evidence or troublingly inconsistent/inconclusive studies at any level

### "All-or-None" Studies (Level 1c)

A special category for dramatic effects observed without controlled trials:

- **All**: Before treatment, all patients died; after treatment, some survive (e.g., insulin for Type 1 diabetes)
- **None**: Before exposure, outcome never occurred; after exposure, it does (e.g., thalidomide and phocomelia)

These observations can provide Level 1c evidence despite being observational.

## GRADE System (Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development, and Evaluation)

GRADE has become the dominant system for guideline development worldwide (WHO, Cochrane, NICE, AHA, ESC, and 100+ organizations).

### Certainty of Evidence

GRADE uses four levels of certainty:

| Certainty | Definition |
|---|---|
| **High** | Very confident the true effect lies close to the estimate |
| **Moderate** | Moderately confident; the true effect is likely close but may be substantially different |
| **Low** | Limited confidence; the true effect may be substantially different from the estimate |
| **Very Low** | Very little confidence; the true effect is likely substantially different |

### Starting Point

- RCTs start at **High** certainty
- Observational studies start at **Low** certainty

### Downgrading Factors (each can reduce by 1-2 levels)

1. **Risk of bias** — Methodological limitations in component studies
2. **Inconsistency** — Unexplained heterogeneity in results across studies
3. **Indirectness** — Evidence does not directly address the clinical question (population, intervention, comparator, or outcome differences)
4. **Imprecision** — Wide confidence intervals, small sample size, few events
5. **Publication bias** — Suspicion that unfavorable results are missing

### Upgrading Factors (observational studies only)

1. **Large magnitude of effect** — RR >2 or <0.5 with no plausible confounding (+1); RR >5 or <0.2 (+2)
2. **Dose-response gradient** — Clear dose-response relationship
3. **Residual confounding** — All plausible confounders would reduce the observed effect (strengthening causal inference)

### GRADE Recommendation Strength

| Strength | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Strong** | Benefits clearly outweigh risks (or vice versa). Most patients should receive the intervention. |
| **Conditional (Weak)** | Benefits and risks are closely balanced, evidence uncertain, or values-dependent. Decision should involve SDM. |

A strong recommendation can be based on low-certainty evidence (e.g., seatbelts — no RCTs, but strong recommendation based on mechanism and observational evidence).

## Mapping Between Systems

| Oxford CEBM | GRADE Certainty | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Level 1 (consistent) | High | Strong basis for recommendation |
| Level 1 (with limitations) | Moderate | Probably reliable |
| Level 2-3 (consistent) | Moderate-Low | Effect estimate uncertain |
| Level 4-5 | Low-Very Low | Very uncertain |

This mapping is approximate. GRADE evaluates bodies of evidence holistically, while CEBM classifies individual studies.

## Other Classification Systems

### USPSTF Grades

- **A**: High certainty of substantial net benefit — recommend
- **B**: High certainty of moderate benefit or moderate certainty of substantial benefit — recommend
- **C**: Moderate certainty of small net benefit — offer selectively
- **D**: Moderate/high certainty of no net benefit or harms outweigh benefits — discourage
- **I**: Insufficient evidence to assess balance

### Cochrane Risk of Bias

Not an evidence level system but a per-study quality assessment (covered in Bias Assessment skill).

## Common Misunderstandings

1. **"Level 1 evidence = good evidence"** — Not necessarily. A poorly conducted systematic review of biased RCTs is still weak evidence despite being "Level 1a."
2. **"Observational evidence is always weak"** — Large, well-conducted observational studies with consistent results can provide moderate or even high certainty (GRADE upgrading).
3. **"We need an RCT for everything"** — Some questions cannot or should not be answered with RCTs (ethical constraints, rare outcomes, long time horizons).
4. **"Expert opinion is worthless"** — It occupies the lowest level because it is most susceptible to bias, but it is essential when higher-level evidence does not exist.
5. **"Strong recommendation requires high-certainty evidence"** — GRADE separates certainty of evidence from strength of recommendation. Context, values, and consequences all factor in.

## Applying This Skill

When discussing evidence:

- Specify the evidence level/GRADE certainty for key claims
- Use the system appropriate to the context (GRADE for guideline evaluation, CEBM for individual study classification)
- Note limitations of the evidence, not just its level
- Distinguish between quality of evidence and strength of recommendation
- Flag when the available evidence does not match the question type (e.g., only RCTs for a prognostic question)
